If your income is different every month, most budgeting advice falls apart. You can't split a paycheck into neat percentages when you don't know what the paycheck will be.

Here's what actually works.

## How do you budget when your income changes every month

Start with your baseline — the lowest amount you've earned in the past six months. Build your budget around that number, not your average and definitely not your best month. If you earn more, great. If you don't, you're still covered.

## List your non-negotiable expenses first

Rent, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments. These get funded before anything else. Write them down with real numbers from last month, not estimates. The total is your floor — the minimum you need to function.

## Use a "pay yourself" system for the rest

When money comes in above your baseline, don't just spend it because it's there. Move a set percentage to savings before you touch it. Even 10% adds up faster than you'd expect when income spikes.

## What about months where you earn less than your baseline

This happens. If you've been moving surplus into savings during good months, you pull from that buffer. If you haven't built the buffer yet, cut everything that isn't on your non-negotiable list until you're back above water. No shame in it — that's the system working.

The point isn't to predict your income. It's to stop pretending it's predictable and plan around that reality instead.
